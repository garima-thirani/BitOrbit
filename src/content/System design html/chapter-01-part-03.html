📖 The Story — Designing Before Measuring
The Estimation Mindset

One number changes every architectural decision.

A junior engineer is asked to design Bitly. They immediately say: "Let's use PostgreSQL, Redis, Kafka, Kubernetes..."

A senior engineer asks one question: "How many URLs are we shortening every day?"

If the answer is 100 URLs/day, a single VM is enough. If the answer is 500 million URLs/day, you need distributed databases, caching, replication, and global traffic routing.

Capacity estimation is the bridge between requirements and architecture. Without it, you're guessing.

📐 Why Capacity Estimation Matters

Capacity estimation answers five critical questions before any architecture is drawn:

📊 Traffic
How much traffic will the system receive daily?
💾 Storage
How much data will we store over time?
🖥 Servers
How many servers are required?
🌐 Bandwidth
What are the network requirements?
💰 Cost
What will the infrastructure cost?
Rule of Thumb: Interview estimates do not need to be exact. Interviewers care about your assumptions, reasoning, math, and ability to communicate. Always state assumptions first.

🎯 Interview Walkthrough: URL Shortener

Let's walk through a complete capacity estimation for a URL shortener — a classic system design interview question.

1
State Assumptions
50M DAU · 2 URLs shortened/user/day · 500M redirects/day
2
Calculate Writes
50M × 2 = 100M writes/day
3
Calculate Reads
500M redirects/day → Read-heavy system (5:1 read:write ratio)
4
Average QPS
Writes: 100M / 86,400 ≈ 1,157 QPS · Reads: 500M / 86,400 ≈ 5,787 QPS
5
Peak Traffic
Peak read traffic ≈ 25,000–30,000 QPS (3–5× average)
6
Architecture Emerges
Read replicas · Redis cache · CDN · Load balancer · Auto-scaling
📝 Production Insight
Real World

Amazon & Netflix Begin with Numbers

At Amazon and Netflix, early design reviews often begin with traffic estimates before any component diagrams. Numbers justify architectural choices and prevent over-engineering or under-sizing.

Key insight: A system designed for 100 QPS looks very different from one designed for 100,000 QPS. The numbers dictate the architecture — not the other way around.

🚨 Common Mistakes
❌ Forgetting Assumptions
Always state your assumptions before calculating.
❌ Designing Before Estimating
Numbers first, architecture second.
❌ Using Average QPS
Always design for peak traffic, not average.
❌ Ignoring Storage Growth
Data accumulates. Plan for 3–5 years.
❌ Assuming Infinite Hardware
Every server has limits. Know them.
🧠 Memory Trick: Numbers → Architecture
Remember the chain: Requirements → Capacity → Architecture → Technology
Never reverse this order. Technology is the last decision, not the first.

🧪 Test Your Knowledge
1. If a system receives 50 million requests per day, what is the approximate average QPS?
A) 289 QPS
B) 579 QPS
C) 579 QPS (50,000,000 / 86,400 ≈ 579)
D) 1,157 QPS
50,000,000 / 86,400 = 578.7 ≈ 579 QPS
2. What is the most important number to design for — average QPS or peak QPS?
A) Average QPS — it represents typical load
B) Peak QPS — the system must handle maximum traffic
C) Neither — just add more servers
D) Minimum QPS — ensure baseline performance
Always design for peak QPS (typically 3-5× average). The system must survive traffic spikes.
3. A URL shortener stores 500 bytes per record with 20M new URLs/day. What is the yearly storage requirement?
A) 1.82 TB
B) 3.65 TB (20M × 500B = 10GB/day × 365 = 3.65TB)
C) 7.30 TB
D) 10.0 TB
20M × 500 bytes = 10 GB/day. 10 GB × 365 = 3.65 TB/year.
